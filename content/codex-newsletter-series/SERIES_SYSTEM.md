This file is the single source of truth for the Codex newsletter series.

Use it to keep future issues consistent in:

- strategy

- recurring workflow

When a durable decision is made for the series, update this file so later issues inherit it.

## Series Objective

Create a 12-issue newsletter series that teaches non-technical business owners and operators how Codex works and how to use it inside a real business workflow.

The series should:

1. Explain Codex deeply enough that hard concepts become understandable.
2. Show exactly how to apply those concepts in practice.
3. Use a recurring workflow so the ideas stay concrete.
4. Move readers toward an agentic ops audit.

## Audience

Primary audience:

- business owners
- operators
- team leads
- non-technical decision-makers

Audience assumptions:

- low prior awareness of Codex
- low comfort with technical jargon
- high interest in practical business application
- skepticism toward hype

## Series Promise

By the end of the series, the reader should be able to explain what Codex is, describe its core capabilities in plain language, understand where human review belongs, and see how to build a controlled workflow around it.

## Recurring Workflow

Use one recurring workflow through the whole series:

- inbound sales follow-up

Workflow sequence:

1. A new lead arrives.
2. Lead details are captured.
3. Relevant business context is loaded.
4. The work is planned.
5. Qualification rules are applied.
6. Company research is performed.
7. A follow-up message is drafted.
8. Risks, exclusions, and fit issues are checked.
9. A recommended next step is created.
10. Human review happens.
11. Final action is taken.
12. Outcome is logged.
13. The workflow is improved for the next lead.

4. one practical build step the reader can follow

## What Must Stay Consistent Across The Series

- Codex is framed as an execution-oriented system, not merely a chatbot.
- Hard concepts are explained from first principles.
- The writing stays practical, calm, and non-hype.
- The same sales-follow-up workflow appears in every issue.
- Every issue tells the reader what to do, not only what to think.
- Human supervision remains explicit throughout.

## Voice

The writing should be:

- technical but clear
- practical
- business-relevant
- calm and direct
- specific instead of inflated
- human, not institutional

## What To Avoid

- generic AI hype
- abstract "transformation" language
- unexplained jargon
- padded setup phrases
- repetitive one-sentence fragments that make the piece feel mechanical

Avoid these habits:

- writing every sentence on its own line unless the formatting needs it
- stacking short claims without development
- presenting concepts as bullet lists when the reader needs narrative explanation

## Deep Explanation Standard

When a concept is complex:

1. start with the simplest definition possible
2. explain what problem it solves
3. show what goes wrong without it
4. break the mechanism into steps
5. connect it to the recurring workflow
6. translate it into business meaning
7. explain the limits
8. restate it more simply at the end

The explanation is not finished until a non-technical reader could repeat it back in everyday language.

## Storytelling Standard

Each issue should feel like a guided walkthrough.

Use this sequence:

1. open on a concrete moment in the workflow
2. surface the tension or decision
3. introduce the concept as the missing piece
4. explain how it works
5. show how the workflow changes once the concept is applied
6. show what still needs human judgment

## Newsletter Readability Rules

- Prefer paragraphs over line-by-line fragments.
- Use bullets only when scan speed clearly improves.
- Keep sections distinct, but let the prose flow.
- Mix short and long sentences.
- Use examples generously.
- End sections with practical meaning for the operator.

## Practical Section Standard

The practical section must tell the reader:

- what to do
- what to prepare
- what the output should be
- what must be reviewed before moving forward
- what they should have achieved by the end of the step

Do not leave critical steps implied.

Because the audience is non-technical and may have no prior Codex experience, practical sections should start from the basics. Do not assume the reader already knows how to frame a task, what to include in a prompt, or how to judge whether a result is usable.

For beginner issues, start before the main business workflow. First orient the reader inside Codex, then show one very small safe interaction, and only then move into the recurring workflow.

Use this pattern for practical steps:

- Step
- What the reader should do
- What the reader should understand from the step
- What the reader should have by the end of it

## Concept Canon

### Codex

Codex is an execution-oriented agentic system. It is not only for answering questions. It is designed to take on defined tasks, operate in a controlled environment, and return work that can be reviewed.

### Ask vs Code

`Ask` is for understanding.

`Code` is for execution.

Use this distinction repeatedly:

- Ask: help me think
- Code: help me do the work

### Context

Context is the information Codex needs to do the right work for the right situation. This includes rules, files, background material, constraints, and workflow-specific data.

### Planning

Planning means turning broad goals into smaller executable stages with clear outputs and decision points.

### Playbooks

Playbooks are the written business method for how recurring work should be handled.

### Skills

Skills are reusable instructions that help Codex apply a method consistently.

Preserve this distinction:

- playbook = the method
- Skill = the reusable packaged instructions that apply the method

### MCP

MCP is a standard way for Codex to connect to external tools and data sources.

Preserve this distinction:

- context = the information needed for the job
- playbook = the business method
- Skill = reusable instructions
- MCP = the connection to outside systems and tools

### Parallel Agents

Parallel agents let different parts of the work happen at once when those parts can be separated safely.

### Sandboxes And Isolated Workspaces

These are contained environments where Codex can do substantial work without immediately disturbing the main environment.

### Reviewability And Evidence

Codex should be supervised through inspectable outputs such as diffs, logs, tests, screenshots, and other evidence.

### Interactive Steering

Codex can be guided while the task is still in progress, not only before it starts or after it finishes.

### Images, Browser Use, And Visual Verification

Codex can use visual inputs, inspect visual outputs, and attach screenshots to support review.

### Automations

Automations allow recurring work to happen without relying on someone to remember every time.

### Security, Permissions, And Admin Controls

These are the controls that define what Codex can access, what needs approval, and how the system is monitored and governed.

### Agentic Operating System

An agentic operating system is the full loop of workflow, context, planning, method, reusable instructions, system connections, execution, review, approvals, and improvement.

## Visual System

### Minimum Visual Standard Per Issue

Each issue should include:

1. one workflow screenshot
2. one Codex screenshot

### What Each Visual Should Do

#### Workflow Screenshot

Show where the lead is in the operating flow.

Examples:

- inbound lead details
- planning breakdown
- review checkpoint
- automation result

#### Codex Screenshot

Show the feature or interaction being explained.

Examples:

- Ask vs Code
- Skill usage
- multiple agents
- review evidence
- visual verification

#### Output Artefact

Show what changed because of the workflow.

Examples:

- task brief
- qualification summary
- follow-up draft
- review notes
- workflow map

### Caption Standard

Each visual caption should answer:

1. What is happening?
2. What is Codex doing?
3. What does the human still control?

### Visual Quality Rules

- Use real screenshots from the actual workflow where possible.
- Avoid generic stock or AI-generated UI imagery.
- Prefer screenshots that prove a step happened.
- Use visuals to reduce abstraction, not to decorate the issue.
